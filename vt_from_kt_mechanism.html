Why it's possible — the latent-V tile identity

In latent-V MLA there is no separate V tensor. The same physical tiles serve as Kᵀ in Phase 1 and as Vᵀ in Phase 2; they differ only in stride.

Vᵀ[vd][sk] = V[sk][vd] = K[sk][vd] = Kᵀ[vd][sk] ⟶ byte-identical tile at offset vd·KT_stride + sk

One row of Kᵀ is one column of Vᵀ. Kᵀ is stored row-major: tile Kᵀ[d][sk] lives at linear offset d·KT_stride + sk, with KT_stride = Sk_chunk_t = 20 tiles per row.

Phase 1 (Q@Kᵀ) already needs K transposed — so the hardware loads Kᵀ into L1. Phase 2 (S@V) needs V with the key index sk as the contraction dim, i.e. Vᵀ. By the identity above, the first vDHt=16 rows of that same Kᵀ buffer are Vᵀ — already resident.

Before paid to copy them out into a contiguous cb_v_in (a transpose on the reader's NoC0). After just points the S@V matmul at cb_kt_in and walks it with the stride.

Cost of the remap: consecutive output columns vd sit KT_stride tiles apart, so they can't be packed into one wide blocked matmul — S@V emits one output column per matmul (ct_dim=1), looping vd. Slightly less compute-efficient, but it deletes a NoC0 copy.

When this wins — and when it doesn't

The trick is a trade: it removes data movement but pays a small compute-layout penalty. Whether that nets out depends entirely on what the bottleneck is.

✓ Good when…

  • Latent-V MLA (v_shares_k_buffer). V is literally the first d_v columns of K, so the identity Vᵀ=Kᵀ-slice holds exactly — the only regime where this is even legal.
  • The reader (NoC0) is the bottleneck. Here download+mcast+V-remat ≈ 23µs equals the 23µs compute step; the FPU stalls at the K-gate (+173µs tail). Removing V-remat lets the reader hide behind the matmul.
  • Large d_v (e.g. 512). A 4×-bigger V matmul means more compute per K chunk, which amortizes the ct_dim=1 strided-matmul penalty and dwarfs it.
  • L1 has room to hold Kᵀ longer. Deferring the Kᵀ pop to after Phase 2 only costs lifetime, not a new buffer — and frees the entire cb_v_in allocation.

✗ Not when…

  • Standard attention with a real V tensor. V≠K, so cb_kt_in doesn't contain Vᵀ — the remap is simply wrong. Path is gated chunked_enabled && v_shares_k_buffer.
  • Compute is already the bottleneck. The ct_dim=1 access raised the pure-compute ceiling +0.069 ms (2.881→2.950 ms). If DM were already hidden, that penalty is all you'd get — a net loss.
  • Tiny d_v. A small V matmul can't amortize the one-column-per-matmul overhead; the strided penalty becomes a larger share.
  • L1 is saturated. Holding Kᵀ live across both phases (no early pop) needs the CB capacity; on a tight budget the longer lifetime could box you out.

Read the bars carefully. The compute ceiling actually moved right by +0.069 ms (the ct_dim=1 penalty), yet wall time dropped 0.200 ms — because exposed data movement collapsed from 0.310 → 0.041 ms. That gap is the whole story: this change is only a win because DM, not compute, was the binding constraint. Flip that, and the same code is a regression.
